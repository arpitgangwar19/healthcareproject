Mind/ Body/ Spirit

Mindfulness is the fundamental human ability to be fully present, aware of where we are and what we are doing, and not get caught up or overwhelmed by what is happening around us.

If we can't manage our thoughts, they will dominate us. The goal is to discover novelties, generate fresh ideas, and escape from everyday life. It is typical. Our mind's main purpose is to think. All that matters is how we manage it. The ability of mindfulness aids in keeping the focus on the here and now.
Therefore, practicing mindfulness is a way to calm our minds and control our thoughts, feelings, and behavior.

Key attitude for being mindful

  • Non-judgmental

    Be an unbiased observer of your own experience. Recognize how you're always evaluating and responding to your inner and exterior experiences.

  • Freshman's mind

    We should be open to learning new things and avoid becoming mired in the comfort of our own knowledge.

  • Practice patience and acceptance

    It is a crucial component of mindfulness. We must be patient as we advance and draw lessons from our errors. This prepares you to respond appropriately to anything that occurs in your life.

  • Trust the process

    Build a basic level of trust in yourself and your emotions. Realize that making mistakes is acceptable.

  • Non-striving

    We are all busy chasing our goals but the aim is to be with ourselves right now, in this moment. Pay attention to what happens rather than attempting to influence it.

  • Let it go

    We understand there are particular ideas, feelings, and circumstances the mind wants to hang onto which perpetually distract us. The only thing we can do in such a situation is to let it go because we cannot control everything or every consequence.

5 Easy steps to practice mindfulness

The practice of mindfulness has a variety of advantages, from reducing stress and anxiety to enhancing our ability to stay focused. The majority of us live life in our own thoughts. As our existence in thoughts lessens and our sense of the present moment takes center stage, freedom emerges. Learning to strike a balance between thoughts and present situations leads to freedom of life.

To engage in mindfulness, schedule a regular time. It has no rules. situation. It can be done in the morning, at night, or during your lunch break. As long as you choose a schedule and stick to it, it doesn’t matter.

  • Firstly, find a method that works for you.

    • Sitting meditation- It is maintaining correct posture while trying to calm your thoughts and paying attention to your breath.

    • Check in with your senses- Pay attention to your body's physical sensations, such as the breeze across your skin, the tingle in your toes, or that small backache. By doing so, you will be more grounded in the present and more connected to your body.

    • Belly breathing- Put one or both of your hands on the area beneath your belly button. Inhale down to your hands and low belly while either sitting up or lying down with the spine in its neutral posture. As you breathe in, you should feel an expansion. Allow your hands to relax down and soften at the top of your inhalation, and then exhale while your tummy softens.

    • Journaling- Writing your thoughts down on a piece of paper allows you to look back on them later. You can use this to spot negative thought patterns and actions.

    • Guided meditation- If you'd prefer to be in a supportive environment, follow the instructions of a qualified guide. You can also attend a live meditation session or listen to previously recorded audio meditations. Here at JIT, we provide a variety of guided meditations that address stress alleviation, sleep, and self-kindness that you might like to explore.

    • Mindful eating- Focusing on the flavor of your meal while eating is referred to as mindful eating. It can help you unwind and enhance your eating experience.

    • Walking meditation- A excellent approach to decompress and experience the present is to go for a walk.

  • Do what you love and give it your all. Whatever you do, be completely present.

  • It's crucial to be patient and compassionate with yourself if you're new to mindfulness. If your mind is busy and an experienced wanderer, it could fight hard at first against any efforts to slow it down or bring it to the present. Start where you are, and with perseverance, you'll end up where you want to be.
